# Customer interview kit — for today's calls

**Use:** Glance through before each call. Don't read from it.
**Goal of every call:** learn whether the thesis is real, not pitch FreeCapT. You're worth listening to *because* you're not selling.

---

## The two outcomes worth having

1. **A clearer picture of the pain.** What do they do today? How bad is it? Who else have they tried?
2. **A signal on willingness to pay.** Specifically: would they pay $15/month *for the paid surface* (AI, exports, stakeholder portal, round modeling)?

Anything else is gravy.

---

## The structure (30 minutes max)

| Minutes | Section | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 0–3 | Warm-up + framing | Make them comfortable. State you're learning, not selling. |
| 3–15 | **Problem discovery** | What do they actually do today? |
| 15–22 | **Solution probe** | Light touch on what you're building. Watch reactions. |
| 22–28 | **Pricing + willingness signals** | Read the room carefully here. |
| 28–30 | Close + ask | What did you miss? Who else should you talk to? |

The longest section is problem discovery, by design. If you're talking more than them in minutes 3–15, you've lost the call.

---

## The opening (verbatim if useful)

> "Thanks for the time. I'm in the early stages of building a cap table tool for [European bootstrapped founders / SMB / your specific niche]. I'm not here to pitch — I want to learn how you handle ownership today and where the friction is. Worst-case, you waste 30 minutes; best-case, I build something genuinely useful to people like you. Cool?"

Then shut up. Let them say yes.

## Problem discovery — the 10 questions that matter

The pattern: lead with open questions, follow up with specifics.

**1. Walk me through how you track ownership today.**
- Listen for: spreadsheet horror stories, last time they updated it, who has access, what they trust.
- Red flag if validation: "I haven't looked at it in 6 months." That's *you*.

**2. The last time you issued equity to someone — what happened?**
- Listen for: lawyer involvement, how much they paid, time elapsed, what document workflow looked like.
- This is where the deepest pain lives.

**3. Who else has access to your cap table? How do they get it?**
- Listen for: email attachments, shared Google Sheet links, "my accountant has a copy" — all signals of the portal pain.

**4. Have you ever tried Carta / Pulley / Ledgy / Eqvista? Why did you stop?**
- Listen for: "too expensive," "too complicated," "had a sales call and never followed up," "they didn't support [our entity type]."
- These are your verbatim marketing quotes.

**5. When you raised money — or you imagine raising — what's the cap-table-related part that scares you most?**
- Listen for: dilution math, allocating across investors, term sheet generation, lawyer round-trips.
- This is where Round Modeling sells itself if mentioned.

**6. Do your employees / investors / advisors know what they own?**
- Listen for: "kind of," "I send them an email when they ask," "I have no idea actually."
- This is the stakeholder portal validation.

**7. If I could wave a magic wand and fix one thing about how you manage ownership today, what would it be?**
- Listen for: anything specific. Vague answers ("make it easier") mean the pain isn't acute.
- Specific answers ("I want to stop emailing a PDF to my CTO every quarter") are gold.

**8. Walk me through what you'd need an export for. Last time you generated one — what was the situation?**
- Listen for: fundraise, due diligence, audit, board meeting, lawyer asked.
- This validates the legal-grade export paywall.

**9. If you had a brand new cap table tool tomorrow, who else in your world would you tell about it?**
- Listen for: other founders, accountants, lawyers, peer founders.
- This is your distribution validation. If they go silent, your viral motion is weaker than you think.

**10. What would have to be true for you to pay $15/month for a cap table tool?**
- Don't ask this until you've established trust (minute 22+).
- Listen for: feature triggers, "if it had X," "if Carta was the alternative" — anything concrete.
- If the answer is "I'd never pay that's a cap table," ask why. Their answer is the disqualifying signal.

## What to LISTEN for (signals)

### Strong validation signals (the thesis is real)
- "Yeah I use a spreadsheet and it's a mess."
- "Carta wanted to charge me [$X] and I noped out."
- "I had to pay my lawyer [€/£X] just to issue options to my first hire."
- "I'd pay [$15/month] in a heartbeat if it just did [specific thing]."
- "Can I see it? When can I try it?"
- They ask to be on the beta list unprompted.

### Weak/concerning signals (re-examine)
- "Honestly I just don't think about it that much."
- "My accountant handles it." (Then your customer is the accountant, not them.)
- "I don't think I need anything until I raise money." (Defers the entire problem.)
- They get vague when you push for specifics.
- They don't bring up Carta or competitors without you mentioning them first — means the category isn't even on their radar.

### Disqualification signals (this isn't your ICP)
- They're at Series B+ scale and already paying Carta a lot — they're not switching.
- They're in regulated industries (banking, insurance) where the pain is different.
- They're solo founders with no employees, no investors, no plans for either — too early for any cap table tool.
- They want features that pull you toward "becoming Carta" (preferred classes, complex anti-dilution, secondary trading).

## Anti-patterns (don't do)

1. **Don't pitch in the first 15 minutes.** If you start describing FreeCapT, they'll be polite and you'll learn nothing.
2. **Don't ask leading questions.** "Wouldn't it be great if there was a tool that…?" produces yes-noise. Ask what they do, not what they wish.
3. **Don't argue with their problems.** If they say spreadsheets work fine, write that down — don't try to convince them otherwise on a discovery call.
4. **Don't talk about your tech stack, your team, your roadmap.** They don't care yet, and it signals you're selling.
5. **Don't show the wireframes unless they ask.** They will ask; let them.
6. **Don't promise dates or features.** "Coming soon" promises bind you. "We're exploring that" is fine.
7. **Don't take a beta signup as validation.** Beta interest is cheap. Pre-paying is validation. (You can offer "lifetime free for early users in exchange for monthly feedback calls" — that's a stronger validation signal than a generic waitlist signup.)

## The pricing question — how to read it

When you get to question 10 ("$15/month?"), the *exact answer* matters less than the *category of answer*:

| Answer category | What it means |
|---|---|
| "Yes, for X feature" | Validation — and X is your messaging anchor |
| "Yes, but only when I have N stakeholders" | Validation, with caveat (they need to feel they've outgrown free first — this is fine) |
| "Maybe, depends on the alternative" | They're calibrating against Carta. Anchor against $2,400/yr — usually closes them. |
| "Probably not — I'd just stay on free" | Could be honest validation of free tier OR signal that paid features aren't compelling. Push: "what feature would make you pay?" |
| "$15/month is too cheap, I don't trust it" | Real signal in regulated/financial contexts. Worth noting but rare for SMB. |
| "I'd never pay for a cap table tool" | Either disqualified ICP or you're mispitching the value moment. Ask what they'd pay for in their workflow. |

## Closing the call

> "This was really helpful. Two last questions: what did I miss — anything you wish I'd asked? And: who else in your network has this same problem who'd be worth me talking to?"

The "who else" question is gold. Each call should ideally produce 1–2 more call leads. That's how you go from 1 → 10 → 50 conversations without ever cold-emailing.

---

## After each call (5 minutes, while it's fresh)

Write down:
- Best verbatim quote (you'll use this in marketing later)
- The single most surprising thing they said
- One thing they did/don't do that contradicts your assumptions
- Did they ask to see it? (Beta-list candidate)
- Did they offer to introduce you to anyone?
- Pricing read (1–5: would they pay $15/mo?)

Keep these notes — they become the customer-research file that, six months from now, you'll wish you'd kept.

## Things to flag back to me after the calls

Based on what you hear, the things most likely to change in the spec are:

1. **Pricing point** — is $15/mo too high, too low, or about right? If three people independently say "more," we revisit upward.
2. **Paid vs free split** — is anyone saying they'd pay for *only* one of (AI / exports / stakeholder portal)? That suggests we could unbundle later.
3. **Country pack priority** — does anyone mention a jurisdiction we don't yet support (France SARL, Netherlands BV, Finland Oy)? That moves up the post-launch list.
4. **Killer feature we missed** — anything that comes up 2+ times unprompted that we haven't speced. This is the highest-value insight.
5. **Carta-comparison phrases that resonated** — for landing-page copy.

Take 10 minutes of notes; send me bullets; I'll update the scope where it changes the call.
